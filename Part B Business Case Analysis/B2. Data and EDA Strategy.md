## B2. Data and EDA Strategy

### (a) Joining the Four Tables and Final Dataset Design

#### Source Tables

1. **transactions** – individual sales records (date, store_id, items sold, revenue, promotion_id if applicable)
2. **store attributes** – store_id, size, location type, demographics, competition density
3. **promotion details** – promotion_id, promotion type, discount depth, category scope, duration
4. **calendar** – date, month, quarter, weekend flag, festival flag, holiday season markers

#### Join Strategy

Use a star-schema style merge process:

* Join **transactions** to **store attributes** using `store_id`
* Join **transactions** to **promotion details** using `promotion_id`
* Join **transactions** to **calendar** using `transaction_date = date`

All joins should preserve transaction rows first, then aggregate into modelling data.

#### Grain of Final Modelling Dataset

**One row = one store × one month × one promotion scenario**

This grain matches the business decision: selecting the best promotion for each store each month.

#### Aggregations Before Modelling

Aggregate transaction-level data into monthly store-level features:

* Total `items_sold`
* Total revenue
* Number of transactions
* Average basket size
* Unique customers (if available)
* Average selling price
* Footfall totals or averages
* Promotion active days in month
* Previous month sales
* Rolling 3-month average sales

#### Why This Grain Is Best

* Matches monthly promotion planning cycle
* Reduces transaction-level noise
* Easier to interpret and operationalize predictions

### (b) EDA Before Modelling

#### 1. Promotion Performance Comparison

**Chart:** Boxplot / bar chart of items sold by promotion type

**Look for:** Highest median uplift, consistency, outliers

**Impact:** Helps create baseline rankings and promotion features.

---

#### 2. Store Segment Analysis

**Chart:** Sales by urban / semi-urban / rural stores

**Look for:** Different responses by location type

**Impact:** Add interaction terms (`promotion × location_type`) or separate models.

---

#### 3. Time Series Trend / Seasonality

**Chart:** Monthly items sold over time

**Look for:** Festival peaks, seasonality, growth/decline trends

**Impact:** Add month, quarter, festival, lag, rolling-average features.

---

#### 4. Correlation Heatmap

**Chart:** Numerical feature correlation matrix

**Look for:** Strong drivers (footfall, competition density, prior sales), multicollinearity

**Impact:** Remove redundant features or use regularized models.

---

#### 5. Distribution Checks

**Chart:** Histograms of items sold, revenue, footfall

**Look for:** Skewness, outliers, zero inflation

**Impact:** Apply log transforms or robust modelling methods.

---

#### 6. Missing Data Audit

**Chart:** Missingness percentages / matrix

**Look for:** Missing data patterns by store or month

**Impact:** Choose imputation strategy or remove unreliable fields.

### (c) 80% Transactions Have No Promotion (Imbalance Problem)

#### How This Can Harm the Model

If most rows are non-promotion data, the model may learn that **no promotion** is the default pattern and fail to estimate promotional impact accurately.

Possible issues:

* Weak learning of rare promotion effects
* Predictions biased toward normal sales levels
* Underestimation of useful promotions
* Poor ranking of promotion options

#### Steps to Address It

##### 1. Balanced Training Samples

Use undersampling of non-promotion rows or oversampling promotion rows.

##### 2. Weighted Loss Functions

Give higher sample weights to promotion records.

##### 3. Two-Stage Modelling

* Model baseline demand first
* Model incremental uplift from promotions second

##### 4. Promotion Features

Create features such as:

* `is_promotion`
* promotion type
* discount depth
* promotion duration

##### 5. Time-Based Validation

Ensure validation sets contain promotion periods.

##### 6. Uplift / Causal Modelling

Estimate treatment effect of promotions versus no promotion.

#### Recommended Practical Approach

Use a **baseline sales model + promotion uplift model with weighted training data**. This separates normal demand from promotion-driven demand more effectively.
