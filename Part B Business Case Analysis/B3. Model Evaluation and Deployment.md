## B3. Model Evaluation and Deployment

### (a) Train-Test Split, Why Random Split Is Wrong, and Evaluation Metrics

#### Recommended Train-Test Split

Since the data is **monthly store-level data across 3 years for 50 stores**, use a **time-based split** instead of a random split.

Example:

* **Training set:** First 30 months
* **Validation set:** Next 3 months
* **Test set:** Final 3 months

Or use **rolling-window / walk-forward validation**:

* Train on Year 1 → Test on early Year 2
* Train on Years 1–2 → Test on early Year 3
* Train on Years 1–2.5 → Test on final months

This mimics real business use: predicting future months from historical data.

---

#### Why Random Split Is Inappropriate

A random split mixes past and future observations.

Problems:

* Data leakage: future seasonal patterns may appear in training
* Overly optimistic accuracy
* Unrealistic evaluation because real deployment predicts unseen future months

For example, December 2025 data should not help predict March 2024.

---

#### Evaluation Metrics

### 1. MAE (Mean Absolute Error)

Average absolute difference between predicted and actual items sold.

**Business meaning:**
If MAE = 20, predictions are off by 20 items on average.

Useful because it is easy for managers to understand.

---

### 2. RMSE (Root Mean Squared Error)

Penalizes larger prediction mistakes more heavily.

**Business meaning:**
Useful when large errors (e.g., understocking during major promotions) are costly.

---

### 3. MAPE (Mean Absolute Percentage Error)

Measures percentage error.

**Business meaning:**
Helpful when comparing small and large stores fairly.

Example: error of 20 items matters more for a small store than a flagship store.

---

### 4. R² Score

Shows proportion of variance explained.

**Business meaning:**
Higher R² means model captures demand patterns well.

---

### Best Practical Combination

Use:

* **MAE** for business interpretability
* **RMSE** for risk of large misses
* **MAPE** for fairness across stores

## (b) Explaining Why Same Store Gets Different Promotions in Different Months

The model recommends:

* **December:** Loyalty Points Bonus
* **March:** Flat Discount

This means monthly conditions changed.

---

### How to Investigate Using Feature Importance

Use:

* Global feature importance (Random Forest / XGBoost)
* SHAP values or local explanations for each prediction

Check which features most influenced December vs March.

---

### Example Explanation

#### December Recommendation: Loyalty Points Bonus

Likely drivers:

* Festival / holiday shopping season
* High footfall expected
* Repeat customers active
* Customers willing to spend more without heavy discounts

So loyalty rewards maximize volume while protecting margin.

---

#### March Recommendation: Flat Discount

Likely drivers:

* Lower seasonal demand
* Post-holiday slowdown
* Higher local competition
* Price-sensitive customers

So discounts stimulate purchases.

---

### How to Communicate to Marketing Team

Use simple explanation dashboard:

| Month    | Recommended Promotion | Key Drivers                                     |
| -------- | --------------------- | ----------------------------------------------- |
| December | Loyalty Points Bonus  | Festival season, high traffic, repeat customers |
| March    | Flat Discount         | Lower demand, price sensitivity, competition    |

Message:

> Same store can need different promotions because customer behavior changes by season, competition, and demand conditions.

## (c) End-to-End Deployment Process

### Step 1: Save the Trained Model

After training pipeline:

* Save preprocessing + model together using `joblib` or `pickle`

Example:

```python
joblib.dump(model_pipeline, "promotion_model.pkl")
```

This ensures same encoders/scalers are reused.

---

### Step 2: Monthly Data Preparation

At start of each month:

Collect latest data for all 50 stores:

* Store attributes
* Recent footfall
* Competition updates
* Calendar flags
* Month / season features
* Historical sales lags

Create one row per:

**Store × Promotion Option**

For 50 stores × 5 promotions = **250 rows**

---

### Step 3: Generate Predictions

Load saved model:

```python
model = joblib.load("promotion_model.pkl")
predictions = model.predict(new_data)
```

For each store, choose promotion with highest predicted items sold.

Final output:

| Store    | Recommended Promotion | Predicted Items Sold |
| -------- | --------------------- | -------------------- |
| Store 12 | Loyalty Points Bonus  | 1420                 |

---

### Step 4: Deliver Recommendations

Push results to:

* Marketing dashboard
* CSV / Excel report
* API to campaign system

---

### Step 5: Monitoring After Deployment

Track monthly model health.

#### A. Prediction Accuracy

Compare predicted vs actual items sold:

* MAE
* RMSE
* MAPE

If errors rise consistently, model may be degrading.

---

#### B. Data Drift

Monitor changes in input data:

* Footfall patterns
* Customer mix
* Competition density
* Promotion response rates

If inputs differ from training data, retraining may be needed.

---

#### C. Concept Drift

Check if promotions no longer behave the same.

Example:

BOGO used to work well but no longer lifts sales.

---

### Step 6: Retraining Policy

Retrain when:

* Performance drops beyond threshold
* Major seasonality changes occur
* New promotion types launched
* Every quarter or every 6 months regularly

---

### Recommended Production Workflow

**Train model → Save pipeline → Monthly scoring → Recommend promotions → Monitor errors/drift → Retrain when needed**
