# Part B: Business Case Analysis

## B1. Problem Formulation

### (a) Machine Learning Formulation

**Objective:** Predict which promotion should be deployed in each store each month to maximize the number of items sold.

**Target Variable:**

* `items_sold` (monthly sales volume per store under a given promotion)

**Candidate Input Features:**

* Store attributes: `store_id`, store size, floor area, staffing level
* Location context: urban / semi-urban / rural, region, local income level, demographics
* Demand indicators: monthly footfall, historical traffic, repeat customer rate
* Market conditions: competition density, nearby competitors, seasonality
* Promotion details: Flat Discount, BOGO, Free Gift, Category-Specific Offer, Loyalty Points Bonus
* Time features: month, quarter, holiday season, festival periods, payday timing
* Historical performance: prior month items sold, prior promotion lift, rolling averages
* Inventory variables: stock availability, category mix

**Problem Type:**
This is primarily a **supervised regression problem** because the business wants to predict a numeric outcome (`items_sold`) for each store-promotion-month combination.

After generating predictions for all promotion options, the company can choose the promotion with the highest predicted sales volume for each store/month.

**Why regression instead of classification?**

* The outcome is continuous/count-based rather than a label.
* Management needs expected magnitude of impact, not only a category such as “good” or “bad”.
* Predicted values allow ranking all five promotions by expected performance.

---

### (b) Why Items Sold Is Better Than Revenue

Using **total sales revenue** can be misleading because revenue is affected by pricing decisions, discount depth, product mix, and premium-item purchases.

Example:

* A Flat Discount promotion may increase unit volume substantially but lower average selling price.
* A premium product month may increase revenue even if fewer units were sold.

If the goal is to evaluate **promotion effectiveness at driving demand**, then `items_sold` is a cleaner measure because it focuses on customer response and purchase volume.

**Why items sold is more reliable here:**

* Directly measures demand generated by the promotion.
* Less distorted by temporary price changes.
* Easier to compare across stores with different pricing mixes.
* Better aligned with inventory planning and traffic generation.

**Broader Principle:**
Choose a target variable that reflects the **true business objective**, not just the most available metric. In real-world ML projects, poorly chosen targets create models that optimize the wrong behavior.

---

### (c) Better Strategy Than One Global Model

A single global model assumes stores react similarly to promotions, which is unlikely because customer behavior differs across urban, semi-urban, and rural markets.

## Recommended Alternative: Hierarchical / Segmented Modelling Strategy

Build separate or partially shared models by store segment, for example:

* Urban stores model
  n- Semi-urban stores model
* Rural stores model

Or use a **multi-level (hierarchical) model** with:

* Global patterns learned across all stores
* Store-specific or region-specific adjustments

## Why This Is Better

### 1. Captures Heterogeneous Promotion Response

Urban customers may respond better to Loyalty Points, while rural stores may respond better to Flat Discounts.

### 2. Improves Accuracy

Models trained on more similar stores usually predict better than one broad model averaging very different behaviors.

### 3. Handles Data Availability Better

Small stores with limited data can still benefit from shared patterns in hierarchical models.

### 4. Better Business Interpretability

Management can compare what works by region/store type and deploy tailored strategies.

## Practical Implementation Options

| Strategy                           | Best Use Case                              |
| ---------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------ |
| Separate models by location type   | Strongly different customer behavior       |
| Global model + interaction terms   | Moderate differences with shared structure |
| Hierarchical / mixed-effects model | Many stores with partial similarity        |
| Ensemble with store embeddings     | Large historical dataset                   |

## Final Recommendation

Start with a **global model containing store/location interaction effects**, then progress to a **hierarchical model** if enough data exists. This balances scalability, accuracy, and operational simplicity.
